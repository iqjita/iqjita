How to Ace Your First Job Interview: Tips From IQJITA Placement Experts

By IQJITA Academy  |  9 March 2026

Getting called for a job interview is a significant achievement. It means your resume caught someone's attention and they believe you are worth meeting. But now the real work begins. The interview is where opportunities are won or lost — and for first-time job seekers, it is usually not a lack of skills that costs them the offer, but a lack of preparation and confidence.

At IQJITA Academy, our placement cell has helped over 500 graduates navigate the hiring process across dozens of industries. Here is the practical advice we share with every student before their first interview.

1. Research the Company Thoroughly

Nothing impresses an interviewer more than a candidate who clearly knows about the company. Before your interview:

  • Read the company's About page and understand what they do, who they serve, and what makes them different
  • Look at their recent news — any new product launches, expansions, or achievements you can reference
  • Study the job description in detail — match your skills and experiences to the specific requirements
  • Check their social media to understand their culture and tone
  • If possible, look up your interviewer on LinkedIn — knowing their background helps you find common ground

2. Common Interview Questions and How to Answer Them

Most interviews, regardless of industry, include variations of these questions:

  • "Tell me about yourself" — prepare a 60-second summary covering your background, key skills, and why you are interested in this role. Do not recite your resume — tell a story.
  • "Why do you want to work here?" — answer with specific things you know and admire about the company, not generic phrases like "it is a great organisation".
  • "What are your strengths?" — pick two or three real strengths and back each one with a specific example.
  • "What is your biggest weakness?" — be honest, but choose a weakness you are actively working to improve. Avoid clichés like "I work too hard".
  • "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" — show ambition but keep it realistic and relevant to the role you are applying for.

3. Body Language, Dress Code, and Punctuality

Interviewers form impressions within the first few seconds. Make those seconds count:

  • Arrive 10–15 minutes early — never late, and not too early (arriving 30+ minutes early can be inconvenient)
  • Dress one level above the office standard — when in doubt, formal is always safer than casual
  • Firm handshake (or appropriate greeting) and direct eye contact signal confidence
  • Sit upright — don't slouch; it signals disinterest
  • Avoid fidgeting — hands on the table or in your lap, not playing with objects
  • Smile genuinely — people hire people they can imagine working with every day

4. Questions to Ask the Interviewer

The biggest mistake candidates make is saying "no, I have no questions" when asked. This signals a lack of genuine interest. Prepare at least three questions, such as:

  • "What does success look like in this role in the first 90 days?"
  • "What are the biggest challenges the team is currently working on?"
  • "How does the company support professional development and learning?"
  • "What do you enjoy most about working here?" (this one also builds rapport)

5. Following Up After the Interview

Within 24 hours of your interview, send a brief thank-you email to the interviewer. Mention one specific thing from the conversation that you found particularly interesting or insightful. This simple gesture is memorable — very few candidates do it, and those who do stand out positively.
